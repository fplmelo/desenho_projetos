Summative Brief

Impact of Habitat Fragmentation on Amphibian Biodiversity

  • Scenario: A conservation ecologist is studying the effects of increasing habitat fragmentation on amphibian populations in a historically forested region that has undergone significant land-use change due to urbanization and agriculture over the past 30 years. Amphibians are particularly vulnerable to habitat changes due to their permeable skin and reliance on both aquatic and terrestrial environments.

  • Research Question: How does the degree of habitat fragmentation (measured by patch size and isolation) influence the species richness and abundance of various amphibian species in different forest remnants within the region?

  • Data Collection: The ecologist has conducted surveys over the past five years (annual surveys during peak breeding season) across 20 distinct forest remnants (patches) of varying sizes and degrees of isolation. For each forest remnant and each survey year, the following data have been collected:

  1. Environmental dataset HERE: This consists only on forest fragments features of interests for the research

  2. Community dataset HERE: This is a species per site per year dataset.

  • Species ID: Species ID represented here as a fictional common name (“Marsh_Crawler”, “Leaf_Hopper”, etc…)

  • Forest Remnant ID: Unique identifier for each surveyed patch (e.g., F1, F2, …, F50).

  • Patch Size (hectares): Area of the forest remnant in hectares.

  • Isolation Index: A calculated metric representing the degree of isolation of the patch from other suitable amphibian habitats (e.g., based on distance to nearest patch, amount of unsuitable matrix habitat surrounding it). Higher values indicate greater isolation.

  • Dominant Vegetation Type: Categorical variable describing the primary vegetation type within the patch (e.g., “Deciduous,” “Coniferous,” “Mixed”).

  • Presence/Absence of Key Predator: Binary variable (0 = Absent, 1 = Present) indicating the presence of a known amphibian predator (e.g., large fish species in permanent water bodies within the patch).

  • Year of Survey: The year the data was collected (e.g., 2020, 2021, …, 2024).

Exam Tasks

1 - Data Exploration:

Your initial task is to perform a thorough data exploration to understand its characteristics, identify potential issues, and generate initial hypotheses before proceeding with formal statistical modeling.

Specific aspects to address during data exploration:

  • Data Types: Identify the type of each variable (numerical, categorical, discrete, continuous).

  • Missing Values: Check for and quantify any missing data points. How might you address them?

  • Distributions: Examine the distributions of key numerical variables (e.g., Patch Size, Isolation Index, Amphibian Species Count, Total Amphibian Abundance) using appropriate graphical methods (histograms, box plots, density plots). Comment on skewness, outliers, and multimodality.

  • Summary Statistics: Calculate relevant descriptive statistics (mean, median, standard deviation, quartiles, min/max) for numerical variables. Provide frequency tables and proportions for categorical variables.

2 - Explore relationships between variables:

  • Explore relationships between numerical variables using scatter plots (e.g., Patch Size vs. Amphibian Species Count, Isolation Index vs. Total Amphibian Abundance). Look for trends, correlations, and potential non-linear relationships.

  • Use box plots or violin plots to visualize the distribution of numerical variables across different categories (e.g., Amphibian Species Count by Dominant Vegetation Type, Total Amphibian Abundance by Presence/Absence of Key Predator).

  • Consider how to visualize changes over time (e.g., trends in species count or abundance across survey years).

  • Data Consistency/Plausibility Checks: Are there any values that seem unrealistic or incorrect (e.g., negative patch sizes, unusually high or low counts)?

  • Formulate Initial Hypotheses: Based on your exploratory analysis, propose initial hypotheses about the relationships between habitat fragmentation and amphibian populations.

3 - Test your hypothesis

  • Now, based on the previous steps, set a scientific hypotheses and test it with one an appropriate statistic model. Remember that sometimes, hypotheses can be tested using different pieces of evidence. This means that you can use different tests and models to collect evidence of your scientific hypothesis.

4 - Summarise your results

Now, just write commenting on your results and how these corroborate, or not, your hypothesis. Connect your discourse with results (tables and figures).
